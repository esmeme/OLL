Theoretical Background

Open Education Resources (OERs)

opentextbc.ca. Open educational resources (OER)

Open Educational Resources (OER) are materials designed for education that promote the use of open content. These materials are available to anyone in the public domain or under a Creative Commons license that guarantees their distribution, use, and modification free of charge (Butcher & Moore, 2015).

OER encompass a wide range of online formats, from online textbooks to video recordings, YouTube clips, web-based textual materials, animations, simulations, digital diagrams, and graphics (Giulia Forsyth, 2012).

David Wiley, one of the pioneers of OER, proposed 5 basic principles of open publishing: reuse, redistribution, revision, remixing, and retention (Hilton et al., 2010). These principles encourage the use, adaptation, and redistribution of educational resources, allowing users to actively participate in the creation and modification of knowledge.

The Importance of OER

Robert Maloy, EdD, and Torrey Trust, PhD. Empowering College Students to be OER Creators and Curators

The importance of OER lies in its ability to transform education by breaking traditional classroom barriers and making knowledge accessible to all. By empowering students and educators to be creators and curators of resources, OER not only enhance learning but also foster key 21st-century skills such as creativity, critical thinking, and collaboration (Trust & Maloy, 2022).

Characteristics of OER

OER encompass a wide range of online formats, from online textbooks to video recordings, YouTube clips, web-based textual materials, animations, simulations, digital diagrams, and graphics, and more.

They can also include assessment materials such as tests with automated responses (Giulia Forsyth, 2012).

The concern for "humanizing language teaching" in virtual environments, as noted by Barcena, Martín-Monje, and Read (2015), underscores the importance of adopting a student-centered pedagogical approach and their active engagement in the learning process.

This concern guides us in designing our Hangul course, where we aim not only to teach the language but also to empower students to become autonomous and critical learners. Through this approach, we seek to foster student participation and engagement, thereby promoting meaningful and enduring learning.

Designing the OER

by Team OERinfo. The Video on the Gold Standard for OER in Online Courses

Stages in the Design

1. Planning:

In this stage, the course structure, objectives, and content are defined. This involves:

  1. Deciding how the course material will be organized (content planning),
  2. What students are expected to achieve upon completion (objectives), and
  3. What topics will be covered (NOOC approach).

For example, in this stage:

  1. We decided to divide the entire course (MOOC) into NOOCs, sections, or modules, and
  2. We decided to focus first on learning Hangul, the Korean writing system.
  3. We set specific learning objectives for this, "Introducing Hangul and discovering the essential rules of writing and reading."
  4. We selected suitable content and activities to achieve those objectives in each section.
  5. Furthermore, at this stage, we also considered the target audience and their needs, which will help inform decisions about course design (Martin & Castrillo de Larreta-Azelain, 2024).

The implications of Open Educational Resources (OER) were also taken into account, as highlighted in Weller et al.'s article "The Impact of OER on Teaching and Learning Practice" (2007). This study emphasizes the importance of adaptation and equitable access to educational resources in course planning. The idea of "adaptation" refers to educators' ability to customize and modify open educational resources to fit the specific needs of their students and learning environments. This involves not only the possibility of reusing existing content but also the freedom to modify and build upon it to create more relevant and effective materials for students.

Furthermore, the concept of "equitable access" underscores the importance of ensuring that all students have the opportunity to access educational resources fairly, regardless of their socioeconomic or geographical backgrounds. This involves removing barriers such as prohibitive costs of traditional learning materials and ensuring that educational resources are available in formats and platforms accessible to all students, including those with disabilities or technological limitations.

Integrating these considerations into course planning not only ensures that learning materials are more relevant and accessible to students but also promotes a more inclusive and student-centered approach to instructional design. By adopting an OER-based perspective, our course aligns with current trends in open education and demonstrates a commitment to equity and accessibility in learning.


2. Development:

Once the course has been planned, it enters the development stage, where learning materials are created. This involves creating texts, exercises, videos, slides, written documents, interactive activities, quizzes, and any other resources needed to facilitate learning.

It is important to ensure that the materials are consistent with the course objectives and are designed effectively to help students achieve those objectives (Atkins, Brown & Hammond, 2007). Therefore, we have focused more on "how to learn" than "what" to learn.

Elements:

  • Objectives: The MOOC's objectives should be clearly defined and aligned with expected learning outcomes. These objectives should provide clear guidance on what students can expect to achieve upon completing the course.

  • Learning Components: A variety of learning components should be included to maintain student engagement and facilitate their understanding. This may include quizzes to assess knowledge, discussions to encourage participation, interactive activities to apply learning, and multimedia elements to enrich the learning experience.

  • Self-Assessment: It is important to provide students with methods to measure their own progress and understanding of the content. This may include self-administered quizzes, personal reflection activities, and progress tracking tools.

  • Teacher Feedback: In addition to self-assessment tools, continuous feedback should be provided by teachers or tutors. This may include live question and answer sessions, feedback on student work, and individualized guidance to address specific challenges.

Format and Visual Aspects:

  • Accessibility: It is essential for MOOC content to be accessible to a wide range of students, including those with disabilities or specific needs. This may involve incorporating accessibility options such as captions, image descriptions, and alternative navigation controls.

  • Engaging Visuals: The use of images, graphics, and other visual elements can enhance understanding and retention of content. It is important to select relevant and appealing images that complement the course material.

  • User-Friendly Navigation: MOOC design should be intuitive and easy to navigate for students. This involves clear and consistent structure, clear navigation menus, and efficient search options.
